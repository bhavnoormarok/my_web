## Hazratnama: S. Rabindar Singh Marok Advocate

**Yes, respected ones**, I am Rabindar Singh, Advocate, currently residing in Jalandhar(Punjab,India). Actually, our ancestral village is Jahangir Tehsil of District Tarn Taran(Punjab, India). Around the year 1900, when the Bars (canal colonies) were opened, my great-grandfather Sardar Gulab Singh Marok and his sons Baba Ji Sardar Arur Singh and Sardar Mal Singh moved and settled in village Chak No. 253 R.B. (Rakh Branch), Tehsil and District Lyallpur(Punjab, now in Pakistan), because they were allotted 1 murabba (approx. 25 acres) each by the British government. This Chak had a large majority of people who had migrated from Jahangir (on the now Indian side), which is why this Chak also came to be named Jahangir. Initially, this area was inhabited by the native Jangli people, who would not allow the village to be established. For a long time, conflict and skirmishes continued. Eventually, they shifted to one side and established a separate settlement. My grandfather Sardar Arur Singh Ji had four sons, born in this order: Atma Singh, Kesar Singh, Hakam Singh, and Jawand Singh. All of them were born there in the Bar. I was born to Jawand Singh on August 15, 1932. Besides my grandfather Sardar Arur Singh, Amar Singh, Inder Singh, and Santa Singh were the Nambardars (village headmen) of the village. Adjoining our village, there was another smaller (village, also called) Jahangir, which people colloquially used to call Luchgarh. Other neighboring villages included Dhaliwal, Nurpur, Nawan Pind, Kang, and Badhala (all now in Pakistan). The vast majority of the population belonged to the Sikh agrarian community. Some working-class families (kamis) from various communities were also part of it, including Boor Singh from Kapurthala on this (Indian) side, who was Lal Singh's nephew, and Khurshid and Allah Ditta, who were Muslims and worked as blacksmiths.

Muslim Shahabuddin alias Sabu and his son Muhammad Mich. I remained a topic of discussion after passing the 4th grade from the village's Imtiazi (distinguished) Primary School, and the 9th grade with good marks from the Islamia High School in Dasuha town (Pakistan Punjab), which was at a distance of 7 miles from the village. I secured a scholarship and a prize by standing first in the Multan Division in the 8th grade. This prize was received in early September 1947, when the riots were at their peak, from the hands of [Sir Malik Khizar Hayat Khan Tiwana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khizar_Hayat_Tiwana), the Chief Minister of the then-united Punjab, during a function held at the school. At that time, in the Punjab Cabinet, Swaran Singh was a minister representing the Sikhs, and Sir Manohar Lal, among others, was a minister representing the Hindus. At the time of the riots, I was studying in the 10th grade. Consequently, on this (Indian) side, I passed my 10th grade in March 1948 from the Government High School, Jandiala Guru(Punjab, India). While studying in the high school over there, my Muslim classmates from primary school included Sardar Muhammad (the cloth dyer's son), Anwar, and Nazar Muhammad. The teachers at the primary school were Sardar Sham Singh, Sardar Sundar Singh, and Sardar Harbans Singh. Over there, the main crops used to be wheat, chickpeas, and maize. There were no wells or halats (Persian wheels). Irrigation was done solely through the Rakh Branch canal that flowed through the fields. There were small wells (khoohis) in the village, but their water was bitter and unfit for drinking. Only when canal water was added to these wells did it become suitable for drinking or kitchen use. Otherwise, for domestic use, 3-4 ponds had been dug in the village. These were refilled with canal water every 8-10 days. The canal water was absolutely pure and drinkable.

When the riots broke out, the elders took the matter lightly. From March to September, for six months, they sat tight in the hope that peace would eventually prevail. Our neighboring Muslim villages were Dasuha, Kokh, and Miani. There was a threat of attack from them. So, in a gathering of all the village elders held at the Gurdwara, it was decided that a night watch should be set up. Thus, carefully selected young men were stationed for guard duty at the outer gates. The village blacksmiths made homemade cannons using the axles of traditional bullock carts. Gunpowder would be loaded into them and the fuse would be lit. Every second or third day, a cannon would be fired to make the neighboring villages aware of our strength.
Giani Kartar Singh was the sole leader of the Sikhs at that time, who did not even put on his shoes in his mission to evacuate Hindus and Sikhs from the Pakistan region. Not only this, but for the next several years, he worked day and night to rescue and bring back the daughters and daughters-in-law who had been left behind over there. He visited our village as well and told us to leave. Finally, seeing no other option, the village elders decided to abandon the village.
On September 23, 1947, a caravan of about 250 to 300 bullock carts from our village and other neighboring villages set out for Lyallpur. We had barely started when Giani Kartar Singh Ji came again with his companions and said, "Stop for now, there is a massive rush ahead." When we returned to our homes, we saw that Muslims had occupied them; however, they did not hold their ground and fled. Similarly, one day when we were about to leave again, we were ordered to wait a bit longer. Finally, on the third day, the caravan set off.

The caravan had reached just parallel to village Roshanwala near Dasuha when 4-5 Muslim Chaudharies (village chiefs), armed with guns and mounted on horses, began riding alongside the caravan, sometimes moving ahead and sometimes falling behind. They kept saying, "Do not fear, we have only come for your protection," but their intentions were malicious. They launched an attack on the rear of the caravan. Narain Singh, the son of my grandfather's brother Mula Singh, was on guard duty on the last bullock cart. He was shot in the chest. A Balmiki from my village and 7-8 other men and women were also hit by bullets. Narain Singh, the Balmiki worker, and 4-5 others were killed. From our extended family, Roja Singh’s sister and his daughter-in-law were also shot, but their wounds were not too deep. Brandishing guns, they forcibly abducted 1-2 women as well. Among those women was Roja Singh’s daughter-in-law, who had given birth to a child on the bullock cart that very day. They also looted the carts at the rear.

Following this, the caravan came to a halt. The deceased were cremated, but sufficient firewood could not be found nearby. When the order was given for the caravan to move, the half-burnt bodies had to be thrown into the canal right there. Roja Singh’s brother, Kanhaiya Singh, who was a retired soldier, was also with the caravan.

Making an effort, he managed to bring 2-3 Sikh military soldiers from somewhere. They fired a few rounds into the air, causing the Muslim mob standing at a distance to scatter. Only then did the caravan move forward. Upon reaching Lyallpur, the caravan was halted again for some time. Here, the Sikh soldiers accompanying the caravan fired into the air. The Muslim police arrived there and started arguing. When they realized it was a protective escort, they went back. By nightfall, we reached the camp at Khalsa College, Lyallpur.
The next day, the matter was discussed in the camp. Kanhaiya Singh took some soldiers and elders with him and went to Roshanwala village. The Muslim Chaudhary of that place was an old acquaintance of the elders; they met him and brought up the matter of the women abducted by the thugs the previous day. He pursued the matter and got Roja Singh’s daughter-in-law returned, who was washing clothes by the village pond, but the whereabouts of the other woman could not be traced.

From here in the camp, my grandfather Arur Singh and paternal aunt (bhua) Hukam Kaur departed from Lyallpur station for Amritsar. We stayed in the camp for a total of eight days. We had plenty of fodder and grain with us. Flour and lentils were also available from the camp, though in quantities less than needed. A caravan of thousands of bullock carts from the Sargodha and Bannu areas also arrived here. On the 8th day, a massive caravan of bullock carts set out for Jagadhri. A refugee camp had been set up there as well in the grain market (dana mandi). It was heard there at the time that a zealous and fiercely aggressive Muslim police officer had opened fire and killed several Hindus and Sikhs.

After stopping here for about three days, the caravan set out towards the Ballo border. We halted for 2-3 days at Buddhe Ki village. The well water there was found to be poisoned. We suffered a lot of hardships here. The grain and food supplies we had were carefully rationed, but even those would be demanded by the Muslim police on some pretext. If anyone lagged behind due to some trouble, they would either be plundered or killed. When my paternal uncle Hakam Singh's son, Didar Singh, went to give water to a camel, some aggressive elements fired a shot at him, but the target was missed and he managed to run away and escape. Here, a truck belonging to the Dogra Military Force arrived, and they sounded the bell for the caravan to move in the morning. They stayed with us until we crossed the border. In this manner, we safely crossed the Ballo border, which was considered an extremely dangerous and critical turning point. Moving past the border, we made a night halt at a village. Here, a father and son who had gone to fetch dry fodder (toori) for the cattle were attacked by Muslims. The youth managed to escape by running, but the elderly man was killed. This elderly man was the real brother of the previously mentioned Kanhaiya Singh from Chak 257 R.B., and he was my maternal cousin.

Moving forward, we made a night halt at village Raiwind. Subsequently, traveling through Kasur, Khemkaran, Sabraon, and Tarn Taran, while weaving the painful tale of a tragedy-laden destruction, we resettled in our native village of Jahangir to weave yet another chapter of life. After staying here for about a month, I cleared the admission test at the Government School in Jandiala Guru and was admitted into the 10th grade. I passed my 10th grade from there in 1948. Three years later, our land allotment slip was issued for village Bau Pur, which is adjoining Uggi-Jalandhar. Then, the entire family went and settled there. In 1952, I completed my B.Sc. in Medical sciences from Khalsa College, Amritsar, and passed my law degree (Vakalat) in 1957. Later, when the Nakodar courts were established in 1961-62, I started my practice there. Around the same time, I got married to Sardarni Satnam Kaur of Amritsar, who retired in 1996 as a Science Mistress from the Government High School, Nakodar. Five children were born to my family, including Sardar Jagdeep Singh Mallhe, Sessions Judge; Sardar Jugraj Singh Mallhe, Director of Krishi Vigyan Kendra, Kapurthala; Jagjit Singh Mallhe, Attorney General, Kapurthala; Dr. Mandeep Kaur, M.D.S., Civil Hospital, Shankar (wife of Dr. Surjit Singh, Nakodar); and Professor (Dr.) Gagandeep Kaur, Lyallpur Khalsa College, Jalandhar. My respected wife passed away 4-5 years ago, and currently, I am spending the evening of my life in deep peace among my blessed family—my son Jagjit Singh, my daughter-in-law Dr. Gurpreet Kaur (Medical Dispensary), and my grandchildren. "I am here today and tomorrow, but the next moment is known only to Waheguru (God)."
